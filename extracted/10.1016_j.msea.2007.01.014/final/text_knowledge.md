# Work hardening in Fe–Al alloys – Knowledge Card

## Material System
- **System**: Fe–Al binary alloys (0–40 at.% Al)
- **Phases encountered**:
  - **Disordered bcc Fe–Al** (15–20 at.% Al): No long-range order; some short-range order possible.
  - **DO₃-ordered Fe₃Al** (25–30 at.% Al): Imperfect/moderate DO₃ order present due to slow cooling.
  - **B2-ordered FeAl** (30–40 at.% Al): High degree of B2 order.
- **Special additions**: Some alloys with ~0.1 at.% B (Fe–20% Al + B, Fe–30% Al + B).

## Processing Route and Variables
- **Synthesis**: Induction melted in alumina crucible under inert gas; drop-cast into 25 mm diameter cylindrical copper mould.
- **Grain size**: ~0.25 mm (coarse grained).
- **Thermal history**: As-cast/annealed state; cooling after solidification was sufficiently slow to allow a high degree of B2 order in Al-rich alloys and a good degree of DO₃ order in 25–30% Al alloys (indicated by strong superlattice spots and coarse DO₃ domains in TEM). (Weak support: degree of order confirmed qualitatively, not quantified.)
- **Sample preparation**: Compression samples (3 mm diameter or 5 mm × 5 mm square, height = 2× diameter/width), polished on fine abrasive paper.
- **Test conditions**: Room temperature compression; strain to 2–3%; strain rate ~3 × 10⁻⁴ s⁻¹. (Weak support: exact strain rate for all samples not fully specified.)
- **Composition series**: Fe–15, 20, 25, 28, 30, 35 at.% Al (binary); Fe–20% Al + 0.1% B; Fe–30% Al + 0.1% B.

## Microstructure and Phase Evolution
- **Disordered (15–20% Al)**: Deformation by single dislocations with Burgers vector ½〈111〉; dislocations are smoothly curved with some preference for screw orientation; weak clustering as incipient cell structures; many dislocation segments are dipoles (confirmed by ±g contrast reversal); narrow edge dipoles and many small dipole loops trailing from gliding screw dislocations are observed.
- **Fe–25% Al (DO₃-ordered)**: Loosely coupled dislocation pairs (superdislocation partials, marked 'p') or loose dipole pairs (marked 'd'); segments remain more straight and close to screw orientation compared to 20% Al; APB energies are insufficiently high to maintain closely coupled superdislocations – dislocations largely glide as individual ½〈111〉 dislocations.
- **Fe–28% Al (peak work hardening)**: Superdislocations composed of two ½〈111〉 partials (perfect for B2, imperfect for DO₃, trailing APBII faults); generally straight and oriented in near-screw directions on {110} glide planes; abundant superdislocation dipoles; dislocation reactions between two 〈111〉 superdislocations with different Burgers vectors on different {110} planes produce pairs of edge dislocations with 〈100〉 Burgers vector in sessile configuration on cube plane.
- **Fe–30% Al**: Deformation by superdislocations (two ½〈111〉 partials); APBI fault energy is sufficiently high that partial separation is small; many straight near-screw superdislocations with stepped structures from reactions; edge dislocation dipoles and loops; dislocation pairs visible are reaction products, not glissile partials.
- **Fe–35% Al (B2-ordered)**: Superdislocations of B2 lattice (two ½〈111〉 partials); partials lie close together due to higher APBI fault energy; many straight near-screw superdislocations; abundant edge dipole segments pulled out of moving screw superdislocations when encountering obstacles; weak remnant fringes from partial destruction of residual DO₃ order during glide.
- **Key transition**: Dislocation structures transition from single, bowed dislocations (disordered, ≤20% Al) to paired superdislocations with strong tendency to form straight screw segments (ordered, ≥28% Al). Dipoles are present in all compositions: single dislocation dipoles in disordered materials, superdislocation dipoles in ordered materials.

## Processing-Structure-Property Chain
1. **Composition → Flow stress**: Flow stress increases with Al content to a maximum at ~25 at.% Al (~640 MPa), attributed to solution hardening and short-range order hardening on single dislocations. Above 25% Al, flow stress falls sharply to ~350 MPa at 28% Al, then remains relatively constant to 40% Al.
2. **Composition → Work hardening rate (WHR)**: WHR (measured between 1% and 1.5% plastic strain) remains low (~1 GPa) for disordered alloys (<18% Al); rises sharply to a pronounced peak of ~16 GPa at ~28–29% Al; declines to moderate values (~6 GPa) above 35% Al. WHR is higher in ordered state than disordered state.
3. **Composition → Ductility**: Ductility is inversely related to yield stress; rapid fall to ~3% at 25% Al; small ductility peak (~6%) at ~28% Al; steady slow decline to ~40% Al. The ductility peak at 28% Al is associated with the WHR peak.
4. **Boron addition**: B addition (~0.1 at.%) leads to only a slight increase in flow stress relative to B-free samples with little consistent change in work hardening rate.

## Mechanistic Interpretation
- **Primary mechanism for high WHR in ordered state**: Formation of superdislocation dipoles (stronger locks than single dislocation dipoles) behind moving screw dislocations, combined with increased tendency of dislocations to lie in screw orientations.
- **Role of screw dislocation tendency**: The tendency for dislocations to lie in screw orientations is believed to be due to strong Peierls forces at compositions near 25–30% Al, not solely elastic anisotropy. (Weak support: Elastic anisotropy peaks at 25–30% Al, but the relative energy increase from screw to edge is only 5–10% less for less anisotropic alloys at 30–34% Al, insufficient alone to explain WHR peak.)
- **Dislocation reaction locks (Fe–28–30% Al)**: Reaction of two superdislocations with different 〈111〉 Burgers vectors on different {110} glide planes produces sessile 〈100〉 edge dislocation pairs on cube planes (e.g., [111] + [1&#772;11] → [100] edge along [010] in (001) plane). These act as strong barriers to further glide (weak support: observed but not quantitatively correlated to WHR magnitude).
- **Alternative theory (APB tubes)**: An alternative theory based on APB tubes dragged behind jogs on superdislocations has been suggested for high WHR of ordered state, but is not the primary mechanism invoked by these authors.
- **Dipole formation in ordered vs. disordered**: Disordered alloys with curly dislocations of mixed character show less prevalence to dipole formation; ordered alloys with screw-oriented dislocations show greater dipole probability. WHR in ordered alloys is higher because dipoles are super-dipoles on superdislocations; in disordered alloys, low hardening is due to free bowing and fewer dipoles.
- **Ductility peak explanation**: The small ductility peak at ~28% Al is explained by the high WHR: material hardens rapidly, delaying strain localization/necking, thereby permitting slightly more uniform elongation.

## Visual Evidence

### Figure 1 – Mechanical properties (yield stress and ductility)
- **Type**: Property mechanical – dual-axis line plot
- **Description**: Yield stress (left axis, MPa) and ductility (right axis, %) as functions of Al content (0–40 at.%).
- **Key evidence**: Yield stress increases monotonically to max ~640 MPa at 25% Al, drops sharply to ~350 MPa at 28% Al, then plateau. Ductility shows inverse trend: falls rapidly to ~3% at 25% Al, small peak ~6% at 28% Al, then steady decline. Data sources: Refs. [2,5,8] and present study.
- **Supports**: Composition dependence of flow stress and ductility; ductility peak location.

### Figure 2 – Work hardening rate (WHR)
- **Type**: Property mechanical – line plot
- **Description**: WHR (measured between 1% and 1.5% plastic strain) vs. Al content (0–40 at.%).
- **Key evidence**: WHR is low (~1 GPa) for <18% Al, rises sharply to ~16 GPa peak at 28–29% Al, declines to ~6 GPa above 35% Al. Two Fe–Al–B markers show only slight increase relative to B-free counterparts.
- **Supports**: Core conclusion that ordered alloys have higher WHR with exceptional peak at 28–29% Al; B addition has minimal effect.

### Figure 3 – APB energies
- **Type**: Chart plot
- **Description**: APB energies (mJ/m²) for APBI (B2), APBI (DO₃), and APBII (DO₃) vs. Al content (20–45%).
- **Key evidence**: APBI(B2) increases linearly from ~15 mJ/m² at 27% Al to ~110 mJ/m² at 36% Al. APBI(DO₃) is constant ~75–80 mJ/m² (26–30% Al). APBII(DO₃) decreases from ~80 to 35 mJ/m² (26–30% Al).
- **Supports**: Interpretation that high APBI(B2) energy keeps partials closely coupled as superdislocations; low APB energies in DO₃ range allow loosely coupled dislocations.

### Figure 4 – Elastic parameters
- **Type**: Chart plot – dual-axis
- **Description**: C44 (GPa), (C₁₁–C₁₂)/2 (GPa), and Zener anisotropy factor A vs. Al content (0–40%).
- **Key evidence**: C44 increases to ~130 GPa at 28–30% Al. (C₁₁–C₁₂)/2 decreases to ~20 GPa min at 25% Al. Anisotropy A peaks at ~6.7 near Fe₃Al composition (~25–28% Al).
- **Supports**: Correlation of high elastic anisotropy with composition range of peak WHR and transition to superdislocations.

### Figure 5 – TEM of Fe–Al (higher Al)
- **Type**: Microscopy TEM
- **Description**: Bright-field TEM showing dislocation network with straight segments (near-screw), curved dislocations, and dipoles. Scale bar 0.25 μm.
- **Key evidence**: Based on context, likely from ordered composition (≥25% Al). Shows straight screw-oriented superdislocations consistent with ordered B2/DO₃ structures.
- **Supports**: Dislocation structures in ordered Fe–Al alloys; straight screw superdislocations.

### Figure 6 – Weak-beam TEM of Fe–20% Al (±g)
